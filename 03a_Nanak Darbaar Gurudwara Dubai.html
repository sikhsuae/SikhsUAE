Gurudwara Guru Nanak Darbar Ji, Dubai

India and the UAE have long shared friendly relations, grounded in both historical trade and a common colonial past. Dubai, known for its religious tolerance, lacked a permanent Sikh place of worship despite a growing Sikh population. Initially, Sikh families gathered in private homes or the Bur Dubai temple, but as the community expanded, these spaces became insufficient. By the early 2000s, a growing Sikh population began lobbying for an official Gurudwara.

Despite initial setbacks, a breakthrough came when the Council of Imams approved the proposal. In a remarkable gesture, Sheikh Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum, UAE's Vice-President and Prime Minister, donated land free of cost for the Gurudwara, a move seen as unprecedented in an Islamic nation.

Construction of Guru Nanak Darbar began in May 2008 and completed in December 2011. Officially opened on 17th January 2012, it became the first official Sikh temple in the Gulf. Built on 125,000 sq. ft. and costing over 65 million Dirhams, it serves over 50,000 Sikhs in the UAE.

Designed by Holford Associates, the architecture was inspired by Sikh temples worldwide, especially the Golden Temple in Amritsar. Paul Bishop and Arafeh Bashir studied traditional Sikh art and architecture to blend authenticity with modernity. The community demanded a 100-year guarantee on the structure, ensuring it would stand for generations.

Today, Guru Nanak Darbar stands as a beacon of faith, peace, and interfaith harmony in the Gulf, symbolizing a new chapter in Sikh-UAE relations.
